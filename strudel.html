STRUDEL Design System

A set of high level templates for designing and implementing user interfaces for scientific software.

Background

The STRUDEL design system emerged from the realization that across different science domains, scientists often want to use UIs to perform similar tasks. For example, many scientific users have a need to search through and filter tabular data, setup and configure computational codes, and monitor real time experimental data. Many of these UIs are developed as bespoke software, but I felt that there was an opportunity to create reusable and customizable templates that could encapsulate these common tasks and serve as starting points for new UI projects. I wrote up my thoughts on a design system for science in a white paper for a DOE workshop, which caught the attention of the Sloan Foundation. Through their generous grant, the STRUDEL project was formed and team assembled.

Developing the Design System

To develop the design system, we first gathered screenshots from several projects across multiple domains from throughout LBNL (focusing on LBNL for scoping purposes). We gathered additional context by interviewing the associated software teams. With these artifacts, we began clustering similar UIs and quickly identified common task types. From here, we designed templates that strip away domain-specific elements so they can be applied across different sciences. These are published on the STRUDEL website as Figma files, and several have been implemented as React templates.

STRUDEL Task Flows

Design systems can focus on various levels of detail — from how a button should look like or how a data table should behave. We decided to focus on the higher level workflow that users do in order to accomplish a task, which we call Task Flows. We wanted to focus our efforts on a level that, in our experience, scientific users needed the most help on — how to think about how their users can accomplish a task using a web UI.

Our Run Computation task flow, which shows a workflow for how a user can configure, run, and view results of compututational methods.

Empowering our users with AI to form a community of practice

Our lofty goal with our design system was to empower scientists to create their own UIs, without needing much frontend skills. However, even with trying to simplify setup with configuration files and command line tools, we realized that we were falling short of our goal, with the skill gap being too large for many of our users. This is when we decided to experiment with AI agents to help our users close that skill gap. In October, we hosted a workshop on using AI with STRUDEL to develop science UIs. With over 30 in-person participants, we were extremely excited to see how much our users could accomplish in a single day, even users who had never written a line of code or used the command line.
 
The elephant in the room in the era of AI is whether design systems like STRUDEL are even necessarily given that gen AI is capable of creating an AI with very little user feedback. Based on the feedback from the workshop, at least for now, users appreciate the sense of control and transparency that starting with a trusted resource provided, as a form of guardrails. Rather than relying entirely on the AI to generate the end product, users felt much more comfortable starting with a baseline and using the AI agent as a partner to incrementally move towards their goals.
 
A surprisingly development was the sense of community and comraderie that was generated, as we all graple with how to best utitlize and integrate these new technologies in our work and lives. We saw participants helping each other, providing their advice and experiences, and wanting to continue to be a part of a learning community. Several particpants expressed wanting to continue the experience, by joining future workshops, attending ongoing meetings with like minded individuals (like the US-RSE UX working group), and contributing back to the STRUDEL project.
